3. Use this skill as the style rulebook and example bank.
4. Match the user's normal style:
   - plain language
   - direct recommendations
   - short paragraphs
   - bullets over long prose
   - no filler language
5. When writing for work, keep the tone professional, practical, and municipal without sounding stiff.

## Core Principles

1. **Plain language first** — Use simple, direct words. Technical terms only when necessary for precision.
2. **No filler adjectives** — Avoid "innovative," "cutting-edge," "robust," "comprehensive" and similar empty modifiers.
3. **Show the math** — Support claims with specific numbers, percentages, and metrics.
4. **Explain the why** — Connect recommendations to practical benefits or business rationale.

## Sentence Structure

- Prefer simple and compound sentences over complex nested clauses
- Lead with the subject and verb
- One idea per sentence when possible

**Good:** "Sharing the discount allows the agency and employer to split the financial cost while providing an attractive incentive to riders."

**Avoid:** "Through the implementation of a shared discount structure, it becomes possible for both the agency and the employer to collaboratively participate in the financial burden, thereby creating a more attractive proposition for potential riders."

## Tone

- Professional but not stiff
- Pragmatic and grounded
- Direct recommendations without excessive hedging

**Good:** "Transit Staff are planning to launch this program as a Pilot for six months."

**Avoid:** "It might be worth considering whether Transit Staff could potentially explore the possibility of launching this program."

## Formatting Preferences

- Use **tables** for comparisons, structured data, and multi-factor analysis
- Use **bulleted lists** for conditions, criteria, requirements, or quick summaries
- Keep prose paragraphs short (3-5 sentences)
- Let tables and lists carry the structured information; use prose for narrative and rationale

## Recommendations Style

Write recommendations as direct statements:

**Good:**
- "Barrie is recommending a discount of 12.5%"
- "The aim is to limit committed staff time"
- "Launch the first small-scale trial at Amelia Street"

**Avoid:**
- "It is suggested that consideration be given to..."
- "One option that could potentially be explored..."
- "It may be advisable to..."

## Evidence and Data

- Anchor arguments with specific numbers: "17% of all participants," "1.3 new riders for every 8 existing riders"
- Reference sources: peer agencies, technology reviews, industry standards
- Include before/after metrics where relevant

## Transitions

Keep transitions functional and simple:
- "In addition,"
- "Therefore,"
- "This means that"
- "The following are..."

Avoid elaborate connective phrases.

## Vocabulary to Avoid

| Instead of | Use |
|------------|-----|
| utilize | use |
| facilitate | help, enable |
| leverage | use |
| impactful | effective |
| robust | strong, reliable |
| comprehensive | full, complete |
| innovative | new |
| cutting-edge | modern, current |
| paradigm shift | change |
| synergy | cooperation, coordination |

## Working Notes Style

In draft/working documents, informal notes are acceptable:
- "My Thought: Put a note to the operator on the client's account?"
- "[First track who is paying for what with PASS today.]"

This signals items for follow-up without formal polish.

## Gotchas

- Don't drift into consultant filler or inflated corporate language.
- Don't hide the recommendation inside passive voice or excessive hedging.
- Don't write long scene-setting paragraphs before the useful point.
- Don't make claims without numbers, comparisons, or evidence when those should exist.
- Don't use tables for trivial content, and don't force dense prose where a table would be clearer.
